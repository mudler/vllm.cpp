## Verdict

**IRREDUCIBLE-AS-MIRRORED — no code fix warranted; no fake fix forced.** The c8
`p99_itl` residual is a low-concurrency-only artifact of our engine's per-step
determinism — the SAME determinism that makes us WIN the c16/c32 tails and
throughput. vLLM's lower c8 tail is a side-effect of its noisier async runtime
accidentally de-phasing co-admitted requests; it is not a capability we lack.
The scheduler and async-placeholder accounting are byte-identical (re-verified),
so there is no vLLM code that de-phases to mirror. The only structural "fix" (a
true async-forward executor) would risk our c16/c32 tail wins and has no
throughput basis; forcing a de-phase (jitter injection / prefill-split /
release-retiming) is the fake fix the task explicitly rejects.

## Evidence (per-request `itls[]`, `start_times[]`, ours vs vLLM, r1 shown; r2/r3 identical structure)

### 1. The stall is START-loaded wave-boundary prefill co-schedule, byte-identical LOCKSTEP in ours

Spikes >400 ms, c8-r1: ours 48 (START 36 / MID 0 / END 12), vLLM 43 (28/0/15).
Both engines stall at request-wave boundaries (first/last ~15 tokens); MID is
zero on both — the decode body is flat. But the magnitude distributions differ:

| arm | 400 | 500 | 600 | 700 | ≥800 | total-stall ms | lockstep_frac |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ours c8 | 8 | 18 | 0 | 0 | **22** | **32035** | **0.77** |
| vLLM c8 | 18 | 0 | 4 | 10 | 11 | 27984 | 0.51 |

Ours is BIMODAL (a uniform ~840 ms band + a 500 band, nothing between); vLLM is
GRADED (440→900). The smoking gun: in ours, co-admitted requests produce
**byte-identical** stall values — req8 & req9 both **843.3** ms, req10 & req11
both 842.9, req12 & req13 both 559.6 — admitted in the SAME step (both at t=17.99 s)
and decoding in perfect lockstep forever. vLLM's co-scheduled prefills are all
DIFFERENT (req8 713.3, req9 885.4, req10 897.0, req11 443.6) and staggered
(req8 @17.91, req9 @18.02 — one decode step apart). `lockstep_frac` (fraction of
spikes whose exact value is shared by ≥2 requests) = 0.77 ours vs 0.51 vLLM.

### 2. Mechanism: 2-full-prefill packing (ours) vs 1-full+chunk interleave (vLLM)

Budget = 2048 = exactly two 1024-token prefills. A step that packs **2 full
prefills** blocks the co-running decodes for ~840 ms (the uniform 800-band); a
step with **1 full + a chunked** prefill (the chunk deferred by residual running
decodes) blocks ~440 ms and de-phases the two prefills by one step. The scheduler
is byte-identical GIVEN the running-decode count (proven: `test_scheduler_wave.cpp`
"1024 + 1018 chunk"; re-verified `async_scheduler.cpp` == `async_scheduler.py`).
The divergence is the running-decode count at the boundary:

- **Ours**: the whole 8-request wave finishes in tight byte-identical lockstep →
  ~0 decodes remain at the boundary → budget 2048 fits 2 FULL prefills → both
  complete the same step → the new pair is itself in lockstep. Self-perpetuating.
- **vLLM**: async-future forward overlap introduces runtime jitter that de-phases
  completions → some decodes still run at the boundary → the 2nd prefill CHUNKS →
  de-phases further. Self-perpetuating the other way.

### 3. DECISIVE inversion — ours WINS the tail at c16/c32

The residual is c8-ONLY. Same binding, r1, ours vs vLLM `p99_itl`:

| c | ours p99 | vLLM p99 | ratio | ours/vLLM total-stall ms | ≥800 band (ours/vLLM) | lockstep (ours/vLLM) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 8 | 557 | 477 | **0.857 (lose)** | 32035 / 27984 | 22 / 11 | 0.77 / 0.51 |
| 16 | 859 | 906 | **1.055 (WIN)** | 421313 / 416521 | 402 / 260 | 0.92 / 0.89 |
| 32 | 927 | 999 | **1.078 (WIN)** | 2203025 / 2274559 | 2170 / 1879 | 0.97 / 0.98 |

At c16/c32 ours BEATS vLLM: vLLM's jitter spawns a large extreme 900-band (157
events at c16, 1764 at c32) that ours lacks, and both engines are equally locked
(0.89–0.98, batch-sharing dominates), so ours' lower per-step overhead wins.
Only at c8 does vLLM's low-concurrency de-phasing (0.51 vs 0.77) beat ours. If
ours had a real async/scheduling deficiency it would lose at c16/c32 too — it
does not. Therefore the c8 loss is NOT a capability gap; it is the price of our
per-step determinism at the one concurrency where waves can drain to ~0 residual
decodes (concurrency 8 == the 2-prefill budget granularity).

## Why no code fix

- **Scheduler / async placeholder byte-identical** (`async_scheduler.cpp:10-76`
  == `async_scheduler.py:19-74`; `test_scheduler_wave.cpp`). No policy to mirror.
- **The de-phase is emergent runtime jitter, not vLLM code.** vLLM's
  `execute_model(non_block=True)` returns a genuine future — forward N overlaps
  schedule/forward N+1 and the sampled-id D2H competes on the copy engine,
  producing per-step wall-clock jitter that de-phases co-admitted requests. There
  is no vLLM line that says "de-phase"; mirroring the CODE cannot reproduce a
  timing property (the CPU-sim of async depth-2 also failed to reproduce it,
  tail-stall-analysis §Phase-1). Our forward is SYNCHRONOUS eager
  (`core.cpp:110-113`, comment "our executor resolves eagerly") — deterministic,
  low-jitter, perpetual lockstep.
- **The only structural "fix" is a bad trade.** A true async-forward executor
  (non-blocking `execute_model`) MIGHT inject enough jitter to de-phase at c8, but
  (a) our deterministic graphed kernels minimize exactly that jitter; (b) it risks
  the c16/c32 tail WINS by importing vLLM-like extreme outliers; (c) no throughput
  basis — vLLM's own async is −0.7 %; (d) it is a major engine rearchitecture,
  explicitly a separate track. Chasing one low-concurrency p99 axis with it is
  net-negative.
- **Forced de-phase = fake fix.** Jitter injection, artificially chunking the 2nd
  prefill (diverges from the byte-identical scheduler), or per-request release
  retiming all either conserve the total stall (cosmetic) or sacrifice
  throughput/parity. Rejected, as the task directs.
